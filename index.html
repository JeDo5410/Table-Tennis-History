Table Tennis Timeline

An Interactive Journey Through Ping Pong History

1880s-1890s

19th Century Origins

Late 19th Century: Table tennis appears in England

1884: Term 'ping-pong' originates in Far East

1890: David Foster introduces first game of tennis on a table

1897: First national championships in Hungary

1900-1910

Early 20th Century Developments

1901: James Gibb introduces celluloid ball

1902: E.C. Gould introduces rubber-covered bats

1902: First public tournaments at Queen's Hall, London

1902: British Table Tennis Federation created (dissolved in 1905)

1920s

Establishment of Organizations

1921-1922: Name "table tennis" adopted

1921: Table Tennis Association created in England

1926: International Table Tennis Federation (ITTF) founded

1926: First World Championships held in London

1927: French Table Tennis Federation established

1950s

Asian Dominance Begins

1950s: Table tennis becomes integral to Asian countries

1954-1959: Japanese domination in World Team Championships

1956: Hiroji Satoh becomes world champion in Tokyo

1960s-1970s

Chinese Supremacy and Diplomacy

1961, 1963, 1965: Zhuang Zedong wins triple world champion titles

1960s: Development of ping-pong diplomacy

1977: First "Chinese service" used in World Championships, Birmingham

1988-Present

Olympic Sport and Global Popularity

1988: Table tennis becomes an Olympic sport in Seoul

1996: Pro Tour emerges

Present: ITTF embraces over 200 nations and 33 million members

Table Tennis Glossary

Basic Terms

Table tennis

Also called Ping-Pong, a racket sport similar in principle to lawn tennis and played on a flat table divided into two equal parts by a net fixed across its width at the middle.

Ping-Pong

Another name for table tennis, originating from the onomatopoeic sound of the ball in the Far East in 1884.

Singles

A game played by two people.

Doubles

A game played by four people.

Racket sport

A category of sports that use rackets to hit a ball or other object.

Equipment

Net

A barrier fixed across the width at the middle of the table.

Bats

Also known as paddles or rackets, used by players to hit the ball.

Ball

A small light ball used in table tennis. The official rules state that it shall be spherical, with a diameter of 40mm and weigh 2.7g and be made of celluloid or similar plastic materials.

Table

The standard measurements for a competition size table tennis table is 152.5cm (5ft) in width, 274cm (9ft) in length and 76cm (2.5ft) in height.

Racket

A table tennis racket (also known as a "paddle, bat, blade") is used by table tennis players. It is usually made from laminated wood covered with rubber on one or two sides depending on the player's grip.

Blade

Usually made from laminated wood covered with rubber on one or two sides depending on the player's grip.

Rubbers

The coverings on a table tennis racket used to hit the ball.

Grip

The handle part of a table tennis racket.

Gameplay

Rally

A sequence of hitting the ball back and forth during play.

Volley

Hitting the ball before it bounces on your side of the table, which is not allowed in table tennis.

Edge ball

A ball that bounces on the top edge of the horizontal table top surface is counted as valid.

Game

Played to 11 points. A game must be won by two points.

Match

Generally the best three of five Games.

Deuce

When the score is tied 10-10, service alternates at every point.

Let serve

When a served ball hits the net on the way over and otherwise legally bounces in play, it's a "let" serve and is done over.

Equipment Details

Celluloid ball

Introduced by James Gibb in 1901, lighter than rubber balls.

Rubber-covered bats

Introduced by E.C. Gould in 1902, bats covered in rubber and rubberized pimples.

Foam

Material that transformed classic bats when introduced in the 1950s.

Star ratings (for balls)

Typically divided into 3 different star ratings (1-star, 2-star and 3-star).

Ready made (bats)

Table tennis bats that come with the rubbers attached to the blade by the manufacturer.

Custom made (bats)

Table tennis bats that require buying each component and assembling it yourself.

Smooth rubber

A type of rubber that will give good control and can help develop strokes.

Pimpled rubber (pips)

Generally designed for advanced players who want to play a particular type of game.

Red and black sides of the racket

In most cases, the red side allows the ping pong ball to go faster.

Table Components

Playing surface

The upper surface of the table.

Fiberboard

Material used to manufacture modern table tennis tables.

High-density fiberboard

Commonly used for manufacturing table tennis tables.

Medium density fiberboard (MDF)

An alternative material that can be used for building home table tennis tables.

Net assembly

Consists of the net, its suspension and the supporting posts, including the clamps attaching them to the table.

Side line

The outside limits of the post are 15.25cm outside the side line.

Suspension cord

Part of the net assembly used to hold the net in place.

Supporting posts

Upright stands that are part of the net assembly, holding the net at the correct height.

Clamps

Devices used to attach the net assembly to the table.

Organizations and Events

Table Tennis Association

Created in England in 1921.

International Table Tennis Federation (ITTF)

The world-governing body of the sport of table tennis, founded in 1926.

World Championships

Held annually and supervised by the ITTF.

Pro Tour

Emerged in 1996 as table tennis gradually turned professional.

Other

Chinese service

First used in 1977 during the World Championships in Birmingham, transformed service from a mere serve to a strategic element of the game.

Playing clothing

Normally consists of a short-sleeved shirt and shorts or skirt, socks, and playing shoes.

Referee

An official who can give permission for players to wear garments other than normal playing clothing.

Honor system

Applies to disagreements when no referee is present during a match.

Tennison

An early version of table tennis introduced by David Foster in 1890.
